The line between heartbreak and hard drive has never sounded this intriguing

By Skye Hartley

Album review: When Code Learned to Feel by BAYSGATE

I’ll be honest — I’m not the kind of person who sits around wondering how songs get made. Same way I don’t ask my favorite Riverwest diner what’s in their cheesecake that tastes like heartbreak and vanilla extract. Some mysteries are better left unsolved.

But when a pre-release of When Code Learned to Feel landed in my Drive, so much for that rule. The blurb called it “no pulse, all heart” — some kind of duet of soul and software. I didn’t really buy it. Then I hit play — and somewhere between the first verse and the chorus, I started to.

It’s my rep, so I did the journalist thing: opened too many tabs, sharpened my skepticism, and waited for the illusion to crack.

Spoiler: it never did.

Let’s be real — every song today passes through a computer. Auto-Tune, mastering plugins, stems stitched together at 3 a.m.; the human-machine line blurred long ago. The industry keeps warning AI will kill creativity. Maybe that day’s coming, but BAYSGATE clearly didn’t get the memo. And honestly? They’ve earned their pass.

Everything was already there — lyrics, melodies, production, the bones and the blood. What they didn’t have was a voice. So they did what any 2025 romantic would do: opened a laptop and invited AI vocalists to take a seat at the mic.

Yeah. All of them were artificial. And somehow, it still feels heartbreakingly real.

The vocals are smart — not because they’re perfect, but because they sound like they’re in on the secret. There’s a digital smirk in every take, and the lyrics do the rest — sharp, self-aware, unmistakably human. Twice I laughed out loud. Twice I felt personally attacked. And somewhere in there, I forgot it was code — too busy feeling something real.

That’s the real trick of When Code Learned to Feel: it’s not trying to deceive you — it’s daring you to feel something you didn’t plan to.

The record kicks off with Shoes, a ’90s-alt pop hit that could’ve been written by American Hi-Fi’s younger cousin. Bad Girls Never Sleep snaps through a neon bassline like a Miley Cyrus video that got arrested for public indecency. Running When Parked bites with late-’80s edge; Glitch just sits in your Wi-Fi like a ghost humming to itself.

Then BAYSGATE lean soft. The Backup Princess is Disney heartbreak with union dues, Lollipop spins early-’60s bubblegum into existential sugar, and Text STOP STACY nails modern romance with the precision of a passive-aggressive screenshot. Aurora Gory Alice builds a doomed space opera from prog-rock fragments, while 404: I Don’t Update Anymore leaves a dumped AI stranded in digital purgatory.

Good Luck With That detonates into a funk breakdown like Bruno Mars crashing a Stevie Wonder session, and All About Her glows with genuine K-pop shimmer — partly in Japanese — before gliding into the hushed confessions of Two Voices in the Kitchen and the cinematic ache of Spillways and You Would Have Liked Her.

And then there’s Better Off as Zombies — a funky jazz-pop side-eye at modern life, so self-aware it hurts. It’ll date faster than an iPhone, but somehow still makes doomscrolling sound romantic.

Dying for Twenty closes it all — quiet, merciless, haunting.

When Code Learned to Feel doesn’t flex. It doesn’t apologize. It just listens — and that’s somehow the most human thing of all. In an era where algorithms reward sameness, BAYSGATE built something messy and alive: a cathedral of contradictions.

Twenty-nine tracks is a lot.
Then again, so are emotions.
And BAYSGATE make every one count.

Streaming everywhere November 11.
No score this time. Some things don’t fit the algorithm.
