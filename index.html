OPINION · CULTURE · THE FUTURE OF POP

The line between heartbreak and hard drive has never sounded this intriguing

By Skye Hartley · Static & Signal

Album cover: When Code Learned to Feel by BAYSGATE

I’ll be honest — I’m not the kind of person who sits around wondering how songs get made. Same way I don’t ask my favorite Riverwest diner what’s in their cheesecake that tastes like heartbreak and vanilla extract. Some mysteries are better left unsolved.

But when a pre-release of When Code Learned to Feel landed in my inbox, that rule kind of broke itself. The blurb called it “no pulse, all heart” — which sounded like marketing bravado. Then I hit play, and damn it, they weren’t wrong. The thing sounded too sharp, too alive — like it shouldn’t exist. So I did the journalist thing: opened too many tabs, sharpened my skepticism, and tried to figure out how something this weirdly human came out of a machine.

Unless you’ve been living in a bubble, you already know AI’s moved from headlines to playlists. The music industry loves to warn that it’ll kill creativity — maybe that day’s coming, but BAYSGATE clearly didn’t get the memo. And if I’m honest, I think they get a pass.

They already had everything written: lyrics, melodies, production — the bones and the blood. But no voice. So they did what any 2025 romantic would do — opened a laptop and invited AI vocalists to take a seat at the mic.

Yeah. All of them were artificial. And somehow, the result still feels heartbreakingly real.

The vocals are smart — not because they’re perfect, but because they sound like they’re in on the secret. There’s a digital smirk hiding in every take, and the lyrics do the rest — sharp, self-aware, and unmistakably human.

Twice I laughed out loud. Twice I felt personally attacked. And somewhere in there, I started saving genres I normally skip.

That’s the real trick of When Code Learned to Feel: it’s not trying to impress you — it’s trying to understand you.

The record kicks off with Shoes, a 90s-alt pop hit that could’ve been written by American Hi-Fi’s younger cousin. Bad Girls Never Sleep snaps through a neon bassline like a Miley Cyrus video that got arrested for public indecency. Running When Parked bites with late-’80s edge; Glitch just sits in your Wi-Fi like a ghost humming to itself.

Then BAYSGATE lean soft. The Backup Princess is one of two Disney heartbreaks — this one with union dues — while Lollipop spins early-’60s bubblegum into an existential sugar rush, and Text STOP STACY nails modern romance with the precision of a passive-aggressive screenshot. Aurora Gory Alice builds a doomed space opera out of prog-rock fragments, and 404: I Don’t Update Anymore leaves a dumped AI stranded in digital purgatory.

Elsewhere, Good Luck With That detonates into a funk breakdown that sounds like Bruno Mars crashed a Stevie Wonder session, while All About Her glows with genuine K-pop shimmer — partly in Japanese — before gliding into the hushed confessions of Two Voices in the Kitchen and the cinematic ache of Spillways and You Would Have Liked Her.

Every pivot feels deliberate, like someone mapping emotion rather than chasing playlists.

And then there’s Better Off as Zombies — a funky little jazz-pop side-eye at modern life, so self-aware it hurts. It’ll date faster than an iPhone, but somehow still makes doomscrolling sound romantic.

Dying for Twenty closes it all — quiet, merciless, and as haunting as anything you’ll ever hear.

When Code Learned to Feel doesn’t flex. It doesn’t apologize. It just listens — and that’s somehow the most human thing of all. In an era where algorithms reward sameness, BAYSGATE built something messy and alive: a cathedral of contradictions.

Twenty-nine tracks is a lot.
Then again, so are emotions.
And BAYSGATE make every one count.

Streaming everywhere November 11.

Verdict: 8.7 / 10
