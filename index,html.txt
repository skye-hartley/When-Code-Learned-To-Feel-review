    <article class="content">
      <p><strong>AI’s supposed to kill creativity.</strong> Someone forgot to tell BAYSGATE. <em>When Code Learned to Feel</em> was born in the mess of real life—ink on fingers, half-mumbled voice notes, nights that ran too long—and handed to a machine just to sing it. The result isn’t proof that AI can create, but that it still needs something human to feel.</p>

      <p>The record doesn’t ease you in; it <strong>kicks the door clean off</strong> with <em>Shoes</em>, a 90s-alt sugar rush that could pass for American Hi-Fi’s cooler cousin if they’d grown up on Spotify. From there, <em>Bad Girls Never Sleep</em> veers hard left through a synth haze—like a Miley Cyrus video that got arrested for public indecency. <em>Running When Parked</em> snarls with late-’80s grit, while <em>Glitch</em> just haunts your Wi-Fi.</p>

      <p>Then BAYSGATE get sentimental-weird. <em>The Backup Princess</em> is Disney heartbreak with union dues—a stand-in singer stealing the spotlight and somehow making it hers. <em>Lollipop</em> spins early-sixties innocence into a bubblegum fever dream so sweet you’ll need insulin and therapy.</p>

      <p>Elsewhere, <em>Text STOP STACY</em> nails modern romance with the precision of a passive-aggressive screenshot. <em>Aurora Gory Alice</em> turns prog-rock into a doomed space opera, and <em>404: I Don’t Update Anymore</em> leaves a dumped AI wandering digital purgatory—heartbreaking, glitchy, gorgeous.</p>

      <p>By the time <em>Good Luck With That</em> hits—a funk detonation that sounds like Bruno Mars crashing a Stevie Wonder session—you get it: BAYSGATE aren’t genre-hopping, they’re emotion-surfing. Every mood, every mess, every hook lands.</p>

      <p>Across the album they slide from K-pop shimmer (<em>All About Her</em>) to acoustic confession (<em>Two Voices in the Kitchen</em>), then sink into pure sad-girl cinema (<em>Spillways</em>, <em>You Would Have Liked Her</em>). Every shift feels deliberate—like emotional muscle memory, not musical roulette.</p>

      <p class="pull">Then <em>Dying for Twenty</em> closes the curtain—quiet, merciless, and as haunting as Lisa Germano’s <em>A Psychopath</em>.</p>

      <p>What ties it all together? Voice. Not just the literal ones that glitch, shift, and gender-hop, but the literary one: one writer, one pen, one deeply human perspective reprogramming the machine from the inside.</p>

      <p><em>When Code Learned to Feel</em> doesn’t flex or posture. It listens. It remembers. It feels. It’s self-aware enough to laugh at its own heartbreak and still make you believe in pop again. In an era where algorithms reward sameness, BAYSGATE built a cathedral of contradictions—glitchy yet grounded, theatrical yet intimate, nostalgic and future-coded in the same breath.</p>

      <p><strong>Twenty-nine tracks is a lot.</strong><br>Then again, so are emotions. And BAYSGATE makes every one count.</p>
